# Broker Firms & Investment Banks: Stock Assessment Methodologies

*Research compiled: 2026-03-08*

---

## 1. Sell-Side Rating Systems

### What Is the Sell-Side?

Sell-side equity research is produced by analysts at investment banks (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JP Morgan, UBS, Barclays, etc.) and independent research boutiques. The "sell-side" refers to firms that sell financial services, securities, and research to institutional clients (pension funds, hedge funds, asset managers). Their research is typically distributed to buy-side clients as a service.

The primary output is research reports with:
- A **recommendation** (rating)
- A **target price** (12-month forward price expectation)
- A **thesis** explaining the investment view

### Rating Terminology by Firm

Rating language varies significantly across firms, but all map to a 3-tier structure: positive / neutral / negative.

| Firm | Positive | Neutral | Negative |
|------|----------|---------|----------|
| Goldman Sachs | Buy / Conviction Buy | Neutral | Sell |
| Morgan Stanley | Overweight | Equal-weight | Underweight |
| JP Morgan | Overweight | Neutral | Underweight |
| UBS | Buy | Neutral | Sell |
| Barclays | Overweight | Equal Weight | Underweight |
| Deutsche Bank | Buy | Hold | Sell |
| Credit Suisse/UBS | Outperform | Neutral | Underperform |

**Key definitions:**

- **Overweight / Buy**: Analyst expects the stock to outperform its sector or index over the next 8–12 months. Equivalent to a buy recommendation.
- **Equal Weight / Neutral / Hold**: Stock expected to perform in line with the sector or index. Not expected to significantly outperform or underperform.
- **Underweight / Sell**: Stock expected to underperform. Often rare — analysts face pressure not to issue sell ratings on companies that may be investment banking clients.
- **Outperform**: Synonym for overweight/buy used by some firms (often Canadian and Australian banks).
- **Underperform**: Synonym for underweight/sell.

**The sell rating problem**: Sell ratings are disproportionately rare. Even at major firms, only 5-10% of ratings are sells. This is due to:
1. Investment banking conflicts (firm may do IPO/M&A work for the covered company)
2. Management access (companies may restrict access to analysts who rate them negatively)
3. Institutional inertia (stocks generally rise over time, so many "holds" turn out fine)

When Enron was near collapse in 2001, all 15 analysts covering it rated it a buy. This exemplifies the structural bias toward optimism in sell-side research.

### Goldman Sachs Conviction List

Goldman Sachs operates a tiered system within their buy ratings:

- **Buy**: Standard positive recommendation
- **Conviction Buy (CL)**: Highest conviction view — analyst believes the stock has the most compelling risk/reward profile. Added to the **Americas Conviction List** or regional equivalents.

The Conviction List represents the analyst's and team's best ideas — stocks where they have particularly high confidence in outperformance over 12 months. A stock on the Conviction List carries more weight with institutional clients than a standard Buy.

### What Triggers Rating Changes?

Rating changes are driven by:

1. **Earnings surprises**: Actual results vs. expectations (beat/miss)
2. **Guidance changes**: Management raises or lowers forward estimates
3. **Macro/sector shifts**: Interest rate moves, regulatory changes, commodity prices
4. **Valuation moves**: Stock has risen above target price (often triggers downgrade from Buy to Hold)
5. **Catalysts realized or missed**: Clinical trial results, product launches, contract wins
6. **Management changes**: New CEO/CFO can trigger re-rating
7. **Industry data**: Channel checks, supplier data, alternative data signals
8. **Competitive dynamics**: New competitor entry, market share shifts

## 2. The Analyst Research Process

### Coverage Universe

Analysts typically cover **5–15 companies** in a specific sector. They develop deep expertise in that sector over years — understanding the competitive dynamics, supply chains, customer behavior, regulatory environment, and key metrics for that industry.

Coverage is organized by sector:
- Technology (software, hardware, semiconductors, internet)
- Healthcare (pharma, biotech, medtech, managed care)
- Financials (banks, insurance, asset managers)
- Consumer staples / discretionary
- Energy (oil & gas, renewables)
- Industrials
- Materials
- Real estate
- Utilities

### What Goes Into a Research Report?

A typical **initiating coverage report** (first report on a company) is 50–100+ pages. Regular update reports ("company notes") are 2–10 pages. Both contain:

1. **Industry Research**
   - Sector trends, competitive landscape
   - Porter's Five Forces analysis
   - Market size and growth rate (TAM/SAM)
   - Regulatory environment
   - Technology disruption risks

2. **Management Overview**
   - Assessment of CEO/CFO quality and track record
   - Capital allocation history (buybacks, dividends, M&A)
   - Management guidance credibility
   - Incentive structure alignment with shareholders
   - Analysts have privileged access: quarterly earnings calls, analyst days, site visits, one-on-one meetings with management

3. **Historical Financial Analysis**
   - Revenue trends (organic vs. acquired growth)
   - Margin evolution (gross, operating, net)
   - Free cash flow generation
   - Return on invested capital (ROIC)
   - Balance sheet strength (leverage ratios, liquidity)
   - Comparisons to guidance and consensus estimates

4. **Financial Forecasting**
   - **Top-down approach**: Start with industry/market size → market share → revenue
   - **Bottom-up approach**: Start with unit economics (volume × price → revenue)
   - Quarterly granularity (at minimum)
   - Revenue, EBITDA, EPS, free cash flow forecasts
   - Scenario analysis (bull/base/bear cases)
   - Key assumptions documented explicitly

5. **Valuation**
   - Multiple methodologies used simultaneously:
     - **DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)**: Model future cash flows, discount back at WACC. Most theoretically rigorous but highly sensitive to assumptions.
     - **Comparable Company Analysis ("Comps")**: Compare EV/EBITDA, P/E, EV/Revenue multiples vs. peers. What multiple does the market assign similar businesses?
     - **Precedent Transactions ("M&A Comps")**: What have acquirers paid for similar businesses?
     - **Sum-of-Parts**: For conglomerates — value each division separately
     - **Dividend Discount Model (DDM)**: For dividend-paying utilities and banks
     - **NAV (Net Asset Value)**: For real estate and resource companies

   The target price is typically derived from a blend of these methods, often weighted.

6. **Recommendation and Target Price**
   - 12-month price target
   - Rating (Buy/Hold/Sell or equivalent)
   - Key catalysts that could drive the stock
   - Upside/downside scenarios with probabilities

7. **Risk Factors**
   - Standard disclosure: what could go wrong
   - Execution risk, competitive risk, macro risk, regulatory risk
   - Sell-side risk sections are typically brief and serve more as legal disclosure than genuine analysis

### Data Sources Used by Analysts

Beyond financial statements (10-K, 10-Q, earnings releases), analysts use:

- **Company-provided data**: Investor presentations, annual reports, guidance
- **Management access**: Quarterly calls, analyst days, roadshows, direct meetings
- **Industry contacts**: Customers, suppliers, competitors (channel checks)
- **Trade publications and industry data**: IDC, Gartner, IMS Health, etc.
- **Government data**: Census, BLS, FDA filings
- **Expert networks**: Primary research with industry experts (GLG, Gerson Lehrman Group)
- **Alternative data** (increasingly):
  - Web traffic data (SimilarWeb)
  - Credit card transaction data (Second Measure, Bloomberg Second Measure)
  - App download data (Apptopia)
  - Satellite imagery (parking lot counts, oil tanker tracking)
  - Job posting data
  - Social media sentiment
  - Patent filings

## 3. Consensus Mechanisms

### What Is Consensus?

"Consensus" refers to the aggregated expectations of multiple analysts covering a stock. It is typically expressed as:

- **Consensus EPS estimate**: Average (or median) of analysts' earnings per share forecasts
- **Consensus revenue estimate**: Average revenue forecast
- **Consensus rating**: Distribution of buy/hold/sell ratings
- **Consensus target price**: Average of analysts' 12-month price targets

### How Are Estimates Aggregated?

Three major services aggregate sell-side estimates:

1. **FactSet Estimates**: Collects estimates from sell-side analysts globally. Used by institutional investors and widely cited in earnings reports.

2. **Bloomberg Consensus (BEst)**: Bloomberg's proprietary consensus, available on Bloomberg Terminal. Widely used by traders and fund managers.

3. **LSEG (formerly Refinitiv) I/B/E/S**: The Institutional Brokers' Estimate System — the oldest and most widely used consensus database. I/B/E/S has been the standard for academic research on analyst forecasts since the 1970s.

4. **Visible Alpha**: More granular — aggregates full P&L models, not just EPS/revenue.

These services exclude outlier estimates, handle different fiscal year conventions, and normalize across currencies.

### What Is a "Beat"?

When a company reports actual results:

- **Beat**: Actual results > consensus estimate → typically stock price positive
- **Miss**: Actual results < consensus estimate → typically stock price negative
- **In line**: Results match expectations → muted reaction

**The "whisper number"**: The officially published consensus may differ from traders' actual expectations (the unofficial "whisper"). Companies that beat published consensus but miss the whisper may still see the stock fall.

**Magnitude matters**: Beating by 1% EPS may be irrelevant. Beating by 10% on revenue while missing on margins may be net negative if margins matter more to the thesis.

**The quality of the beat**: A McKinsey study found missing estimates by 1% leads to only a ~0.2% share price decline in the 5 days after announcement. However, the nature of the miss (operational vs. one-time) matters more than the number.

The Molson Coors example illustrates this: the company beat consensus by 2% in 2010 due to a one-time tax benefit, but the stock still fell 7% because investors attributed the beat to an accounting item, not operational improvement.

## 4. Conviction Lists and Best Ideas

### How Conviction Lists Work

Major banks maintain tiered recommendation systems that differentiate standard buys from their highest-conviction ideas:

- **Goldman Sachs Americas Conviction List**: ~30–40 stocks. Added by senior analysts after internal debate. Carries significantly more weight with institutional clients. Conviction Buy additions often cause immediate stock price moves.

- **Morgan Stanley "Catalyst-Driven Ideas"**: Short-term, catalyst-specific trades flagged above standard coverage.

- **JP Morgan "Analyst Focus List"**: Curated best ideas across coverage.

- **UBS "Most Preferred"**: Stocks with highest conviction within sector coverage.

### What Differentiates a Conviction Buy from a Standard Buy?

1. **Stronger fundamental view**: Analyst has higher certainty about the investment thesis
2. **Bigger upside**: Conviction buys typically have target prices representing >20-30% upside vs. 10-15% for standard buys
3. **Near-term catalysts**: Clear upcoming events that could realize the value
4. **Differentiated view**: Analyst's view is contrarian vs. consensus or captures something the market hasn't priced
5. **Internal sign-off**: Added to conviction list requires broader team/management approval, not just the covering analyst
6. **Client impact**: Buying from prime brokerage clients often follows conviction list additions

## 5. Report Structure and Client Communication

### Types of Research Reports

1. **Initiating Coverage** (IC): First report. 50–100 pages. Full industry and company analysis. Establishes the analyst's view.

2. **Company Update / Flash Note**: Triggered by earnings, news events. 2–5 pages. Quick reaction with updated estimates and target.

3. **Earnings Preview**: Published before quarterly earnings. Sets expectations, highlights what to watch.

4. **Earnings Review**: Post-earnings reaction. Assesses beat/miss, updates model, confirms or changes rating.

5. **Sector Report**: Thematic analysis across the sector. Industry trends, competitive positioning.

6. **Morning Note**: Pre-market daily note. Brief, covers news flow, market moves, and analyst-specific updates. Published before market open (~5:30-7:00 AM).

7. **Deep Dive / Thematic Report**: Focused analysis on a specific topic (e.g., "AI impact on healthcare", "EV battery supply chain").

### Report Page 1 Structure

The first page of any sell-side report is heavily standardized:

- **Company name, ticker, exchange**
- **Rating** (with color coding: Green=Buy, Yellow=Hold, Red=Sell)
- **Target price** and current price
- **Upside/downside** (% to target)
- **Key financial estimates**: Revenue, EBITDA, EPS for current and next 2-3 years
- **Brief thesis** (1-2 sentences)
- **Catalyst** (key upcoming event)
- **Analyst name and contact**

### How Uncertainty Is Handled

Sell-side reports handle uncertainty conservatively due to legal/compliance constraints:

1. **Scenario analysis**: Bull/base/bear cases with different target prices (e.g., bear $40, base $65, bull $90)
2. **Confidence language**: "We think...", "We believe...", "Our model suggests..."
3. **Risk factors section**: Standard legal disclosure listing operational, competitive, macro risks
4. **Rating band**: Using "Hold" instead of "Sell" when uncertain
5. **Target price revisions**: Frequently adjusting targets to reduce apparent error

### Visuals Standard in Reports

- Price chart (often with prior rating changes overlaid)
- Financial summary table (income statement, balance sheet, cash flow — key metrics)
- Valuation summary (EV/EBITDA, P/E vs. peers)
- Comparable company tables
- Sector/market share charts
- Revenue breakdown by product/geography
- Margin trend charts
- DCF sensitivity tables (output vs. WACC and growth assumptions)

## 6. Sector and Industry Coverage

### Why Specialization Matters

Analysts who specialize in a sector for 5–10+ years develop:
- Deep knowledge of company economics and metrics
- Relationships with management at covered companies
- Industry contacts (customers, suppliers, competitors)
- Historical context for how the sector behaves in different macro environments
- Proprietary frameworks for valuing sector-specific assets

For example, a semiconductor analyst understands:
- Design cycles, wafer fabrication economics, EDA software
- Inventory cycles and how they translate to revenue volatility
- Why P/E multiples expand/contract with cycle position

### The Institutional Investor Rankings

Analysts are ranked annually by **Institutional Investor** magazine based on votes from buy-side clients. The **"II-ranked analyst"** designation is highly prestigious and commands:
- Higher compensation
- More client attention and trading flow
- "Franchise value" (buy-side managers follow the analyst, not the bank)

JP Morgan employs ~870+ analysts across 25 countries covering 5,200+ companies. Rankings like these reflect the depth and quality of institutional relationships.

## 7. Modern Evolution: MiFID II, AI, and Alternative Data

### MiFID II Research Unbundling (2018)

The **Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II (MiFID II)**, effective January 2018 in the EU, fundamentally changed how sell-side research is paid for:

**Before MiFID II**: Research was paid for through trading commissions ("soft dollars"). Banks offered research "for free" to buy-side clients in exchange for routing order flow through the bank's trading desk.

**After MiFID II**: Research must be **explicitly priced and purchased separately** from execution services. Research fees must be paid from the asset manager's own P&L or from dedicated research payment accounts (RPAs).

**Consequences**:
1. **Dramatic decline in sell-side research revenue**: Many buy-side firms cut research spending by 20-40%.
2. **Consolidation**: Smaller coverage of less liquid stocks. Smaller companies lost analyst coverage.
3. **Rise of independent research**: Research boutiques selling directly to buy-side.
4. **Democratization of research access**: Individual investors now can access research through data platforms.
5. **Platforms emerged**: Research Exchange (ResEx) and similar marketplaces for individual report purchase.

### AI and Machine Learning in Research

Major banks are integrating AI tools:

1. **JP Morgan**: Uses "big data analysis, machine learning and other techniques" in research. JPMaQS platform ("Macrosynergy Quantamental System") combines fundamental and quantitative research.
2. **Natural language processing**: AI tools that parse earnings calls, news, regulatory filings, and social media.
3. **Alternative data integration**: Systematic ingestion of credit card data, satellite imagery, web scraping.
4. **Model automation**: Some financial model updates are now automated (updating templates with new earnings data).
5. **Analyst augmentation, not replacement**: AI handles data gathering and initial model updates; analysts focus on interpretation, relationships, and communication.

### Alternative Data Transformation

The rise of alternative data has created a two-speed research market:

**Traditional data** (available to all):
- SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K)
- Earnings releases and calls
- Management guidance
- Industry surveys

**Alternative data** (creates edge):
- **Transaction data**: Credit/debit card spending aggregates (Second Measure, Earnest Research)
- **App/web data**: App download trends, website traffic, search trends
- **Geolocation**: Mobile phone location data (store visits, competitor traffic)
- **Satellite/aerial**: Parking lot occupancy, oil tanker positions, crop health
- **Job postings**: Signal for R&D investment, headcount plans, strategic direction
- **Patent data**: R&D pipeline signals
- **Social media/news sentiment**: Real-time sentiment analysis
- **Supply chain signals**: Shipping manifests, port data

Large firms like JP Morgan, Goldman Sachs, and Morgan Stanley have dedicated data science and alternative data teams integrated into their research platforms.

### Current Trends (2024-2026)

1. **Shrinking sell-side headcount**: MiFID II + automation has reduced analyst numbers, especially at mid-tier banks.

2. **Focus on fewer, higher-quality names**: Analysts increasingly concentrate on larger-cap, more liquid stocks where clients will pay for research.

3. **Rise of independent research**: Firms like Morningstar, CFRA, New Street Research, and sector-specialist boutiques growing.

4. **Real-time research**: Morning notes delivered pre-market, flash notes via Slack/Teams integrations with Bloomberg.

5. **Client-specific research**: Increasing customization — large hedge funds getting bespoke analysis, not just standardized reports.

6. **ESG integration**: Environmental, social, and governance factors now standard inputs into valuation (discount rate adjustments, scenario analysis for regulatory risk).

7. **AI-generated first drafts**: Some firms use AI to generate initial earnings note drafts, which analysts then edit and publish.

## Key Insights for Investment Platform Design

### What Sell-Side Research Does Well

1. **Consistent framework**: Standard report structure makes information comparable across companies and time
2. **Management access**: Regular conversation with management adds qualitative insight unavailable from filings
3. **Sector expertise**: Deep pattern recognition from covering same sector for years
4. **Earnings preview/review cycle**: Timely analysis around catalysts
5. **Target price discipline**: Forces explicit valuation claim (even if crude)

### What Sell-Side Research Does Poorly

1. **Conflicts of interest**: Investment banking relationships bias toward bullishness
2. **Herding**: Analysts cluster around consensus; contrarian views are career-risky
3. **Short-term focus**: Quarterly earnings cycle dominates; long-term structural questions less covered
4. **Limited downside analysis**: Sell ratings and detailed bear cases are rare
5. **Point estimate delusion**: Single target price implies false precision
6. **Slow to change**: Rating changes lag reality (stock already moved before analyst acts)
7. **Availability bias**: Coverage concentrated in large-cap, liquid names

### What This Means for HB (Haute-Banque)

The HB agent system should incorporate sell-side methodology as **one input** but critically evaluate it:

- **Use consensus as a baseline**: Where is the market's current expectation (consensus EPS, revenue)?
- **Identify consensus divergence**: When HB agents disagree with consensus, that's where alpha potential exists
- **Track analyst rating changes**: Upgrades/downgrades from major houses are market-moving catalysts
- **Monitor target price distribution**: Spread between high and low target prices signals uncertainty
- **Flag conviction list additions**: Goldman Conviction Buy additions reliably move stocks
- **Incorporate alternative data signals**: The edge comes from data sources sell-side still underweights
- **Apply sell-side framework**: DCF + comps + sum-of-parts as valuation toolkit, but with honest scenario ranges rather than single point targets
